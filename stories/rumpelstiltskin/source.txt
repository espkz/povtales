Once there was a poor miller who had a beautiful daughter. One day he had to go speak to the King, and wanting to seem important, he said, "I have a daughter who can spin straw into gold." The King's eyes lit up. "That's a skill I'd like to see. Bring her to my palace tomorrow, and I'll put her to the test."

When the girl arrived, the King led her to a room piled high with straw. He gave her a spinning wheel and a reel and said, "Get to work. If you haven't spun all this straw into gold by morning, you will die." Then he locked the door and left her alone.

The poor miller's daughter sat there, completely lost. She had no idea how to spin straw into gold. She grew more and more frightened until she began to cry.

Suddenly the door flew open, and in walked a strange little man. "Good evening, young lady. Why are you crying?" "I have to spin this straw into gold," she said, "and I don't know how." "What will you give me if I do it for you?" asked the little man. "My necklace," said the girl. The little man took the necklace, sat down at the spinning wheel, and — whirr, whirr, whirr — three spins and the reel was full. He put on another reel — whirr, whirr, whirr — and that one was full too. He kept going all night, and by morning every bit of straw had been spun into gold.

At dawn the King arrived. When he saw the gold, he was thrilled — but it only made him greedier. He had the girl taken to a bigger room, also packed with straw, and told her to spin it all into gold by morning if she wanted to live. The girl didn't know what to do, and she was crying again when the door opened and the little man appeared. "What will you give me if I spin this straw into gold?" he asked. "The ring on my finger," said the girl. The little man took the ring, sat down at the wheel, and by morning every strand of straw had been turned to glittering gold.

The King was overjoyed, but he still didn't have enough gold. He brought the girl to an even larger room stuffed with straw and said, "Spin this tonight, and if you succeed, you shall become my wife." He thought to himself, "She may be a miller's daughter, but I couldn't find a richer wife in the whole world."

When the girl was alone, the little man appeared a third time. "What will you give me if I spin the straw for you again?" he asked. "I have nothing left to give," she said. "Then promise me this: when you become Queen, you will give me your first child." The girl thought, "Who knows if that will ever happen?" And since she had no other choice, she made the promise. Once again, the little man spun all the straw into gold.

When the King came in the morning and found everything just as he wished, he married her, and the pretty miller's daughter became Queen.

A year later, she had a beautiful baby. She had forgotten all about the little man — until he suddenly appeared in her room. "Now give me what you promised," he said. The Queen was horrified. She offered him all the riches of the kingdom if he would let her keep the child. But the little man shook his head. "No. Something alive is worth more to me than all the treasure in the world." The Queen began to weep so bitterly that the little man took pity on her. "I'll give you three days," he said. "If you can guess my name by then, you may keep your child."

The Queen stayed up all night, thinking of every name she had ever heard. She also sent a messenger across the land to collect any names he could find. When the little man came the next day, she tried them all — Caspar, Melchior, Balthazar — and every other name she knew. But to each one, the little man said, "That is not my name."

On the second day, she had her servants ask around the neighborhood for unusual names. She tried the strangest ones she could find: "Is your name Shortribs? Sheepshanks? Spindleshanks?" But each time, the little man answered, "That is not my name."

On the third day, the messenger came back and said, "I couldn't find a single new name. But as I came to a high mountain at the edge of the forest, where the fox and the hare say goodnight to each other, I saw a little hut. A fire was burning in front of it, and a funny little man was dancing around the fire, hopping on one leg and singing:

"Today I bake, tomorrow I brew,
The next I'll have the young Queen's child.
Ha! Glad am I that no one knew
That Rumpelstiltskin I am styled."

You can imagine how happy the Queen was when she heard that name! When the little man came in and asked, "Now, my dear Queen, what is my name?" she first said, "Is it Conrad?" "No." "Is it Harry?" "No."

"Could it possibly be... Rumpelstiltskin?"

"The devil told you that! The devil told you that!" the little man shrieked. He was so furious that he stamped his right foot into the ground so hard that his whole leg sank in. Then he grabbed his left leg with both hands and pulled so hard that he tore himself right in two.
