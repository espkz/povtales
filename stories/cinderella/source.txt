Once upon a time, in a great house at the edge of a forest, there lived a kind and gentle little girl. Her mother loved her with all her heart. But one winter, her mother grew very ill, and one quiet evening she called the little girl to her bedside.

"Dear child," she whispered, "always be kind, always be good, and a loving spirit will watch over you. Whenever the wind stirs the leaves, that will be me, smiling down on you from above." Then she gave her daughter one last sweet kiss, and was gone.

Every morning the little girl visited her mother's grave with fresh flowers, and she did her best to stay as kind and good as her mother had asked. When winter blanketed the meadow in soft snow, and again when spring chased the snow away, she still went to sit by the grave and sing the songs her mother had loved.

One day her father came home and told her, with a hopeful smile, that he had married again. The new wife arrived with two grown daughters of her own. They were dressed in shimmering silks and had pretty faces, but their hearts were proud and their tongues were sharp.

"Is the silly little goose to sit in the parlor with us?" they laughed. "If she wants to stay in this house, she can earn her keep. Off to the kitchen!"

They took the little girl's pretty dresses and gave her an old grey gown and wooden clogs to wear. From morning until night they made her sweep the floors, carry the water, light the fires, and wash the dishes. When they were tired of teasing her, they tipped her bowl of peas and lentils into the ashes of the hearth and giggled while she patiently picked them out, one by one. When her work was done she had no bed to rest in, but curled up on the warm hearthstones beside the fire. The cinders dusted her cheeks until they were always a little smudged, and so the sisters laughed and called her Cinderella.

One day her father went to the great market in town. "What shall I bring back for you, my dears?" he asked. "Beautiful dresses!" cried the first stepsister. "Pearls and jewels!" cried the second. "And you, Cinderella?" said her father gently. "Father," she said, "please bring me the first little branch that brushes against your hat on your way home."

So the father bought beautiful dresses, pearls, and jewels for the two stepsisters. And as he rode home through a green wood, a hazel twig brushed his hat just as Cinderella had said. He broke off the branch and tucked it carefully into his coat. When he gave it to Cinderella, her eyes shone like stars. She carried the branch to her mother's grave and planted it gently in the earth. She watered it with her tears, and she watered it with her songs, and to her wonder it took root and began to grow. Soon a beautiful little tree spread its leaves over the grave.

Three times a day Cinderella went and sat beneath the tree, and a little white bird with feathers like fresh snow came to listen. Whenever she whispered a wish, the kind little bird would flutter its wings and bring her whatever her heart needed.

One sunny morning a herald rode through the kingdom blowing a silver trumpet. "Hear ye! Hear ye!" he called. "The King will hold a great festival of three days and three nights at the palace, and every young lady in the land is invited! For our prince is looking for a bride!"

The two stepsisters squealed with delight. "Cinderella!" they shouted. "Comb our hair! Brush our shoes! Fasten our buckles! We are going to the King's ball!"

Cinderella did everything they asked, but as she worked her eyes grew wet, for she dearly wished to go to the festival too. "Please, stepmother," she asked softly, "may I go to the dance?"

"You, Cinderella?" The stepmother laughed. "You are dusty and ragged. You have no dress and no dancing slippers, and you would only be in the way." But Cinderella asked again, so kindly that at last the stepmother said, "Very well. I will tip a bowl of lentils into the ashes. If you can pick them all out in two hours, you may come with us."

Cinderella ran out into the garden and lifted her face to the sky.

Come, kind doves, come, gentle finches,
Come down from every tree —
Help me sort the lentils,
As quickly as can be.

And from the rooftops and the willows, two snow-white doves came fluttering through the kitchen window, and after them turtle-doves, and after them every little bird beneath the sky. They settled in the ashes and began to pick, pick, pick, pick, sorting the good lentils into the bowl. Before an hour had passed, every lentil was in its place. Cinderella laughed and clapped her hands as the birds whirled away into the sunshine.

She carried the bowl to her stepmother with a hopeful smile. But the stepmother frowned. "No, Cinderella. You still have no dress, and you cannot dance. Everyone would laugh at you. Pick out two bowls of lentils in one hour, and then perhaps —"

So the stepmother tipped two bowls of lentils into the ashes, certain it could not be done. But Cinderella ran into the garden and called again,

Come, kind doves, come, gentle finches,
Come down from every tree —
Help me sort the lentils,
As quickly as can be.

Once more the white doves came whirring in, with the turtle-doves and all the little birds of the air, and they nodded their heads and began to pick, pick, pick, pick, until in less than half an hour the bowls were full of plump good lentils. Cinderella ran joyfully to her stepmother. But the stepmother only sniffed and said, "It is no use, Cinderella. You cannot come. You have nothing to wear, and we should be ashamed of you." And she swept out of the door with her two proud daughters, leaving Cinderella alone.

When the great house was quiet, Cinderella crept out to the hazel tree on her mother's grave. She rested her cheek against its smooth bark and whispered,

Shiver and quiver, my little tree,
Silver and gold throw down over me.

And the kind white bird flew down through the leaves and dropped into her arms a gown of silver and gold that shimmered like moonlight on water, and a pair of dancing slippers stitched with silver thread. Cinderella slipped them on and hurried away to the palace, her heart fluttering like a butterfly.

When she stepped into the great hall, the music seemed to hush. The stepsisters and the stepmother did not know her at all and whispered that she must be a princess from a faraway land. The Prince saw her across the room and his eyes lit up. He took her hand and led her into the dance, and he would dance with no one else. Whenever another lord came near, he said only, "This lady is my partner."

They danced and danced. But when the candles burned low, Cinderella remembered that she must be home before her stepfamily, and she slipped away. The Prince followed, hoping to learn her name. But she darted into the moonlit garden and vanished behind the dovecote. When her father came home, the Prince told him, "The lovely stranger has hidden in your dovecote." Her father puzzled and wondered, "Could it be Cinderella?" But when he peeked inside, no one was there. For Cinderella had hopped quickly down the other side, raced to the hazel tree, and given the silver-and-gold dress back to the white bird. By the time her stepfamily came home, she was curled by the hearth in her grey gown, as though she had never moved.

The next evening, when the festival began again, Cinderella ran once more to the hazel tree.

Shiver and quiver, my little tree,
Silver and gold throw down over me.

And the kind bird dropped her a gown more dazzling than the first, embroidered with stars. When Cinderella entered the ballroom, every guest gasped at her beauty. The Prince had been waiting by the door and took her hand at once. All night he danced with no one but her, and whenever others came to ask, he said softly, "She is my partner."

When the music slowed and Cinderella ran for home, the Prince followed close behind, but again she escaped him. This time she slipped behind the great pear tree in the garden and climbed quick as a squirrel into its branches. The Prince called her father once more. "She has climbed the pear tree!" he said. The father wondered, "Could it be Cinderella?" But when they shook the branches, no one was there. For Cinderella had jumped down on the other side, returned the dress to the white bird, and slipped home in her grey gown.

On the third night, Cinderella went a third time to the hazel tree.

Shiver and quiver, my little tree,
Silver and gold throw down over me.

And now the bird brought her a dress more wonderful than any she had yet worn — a gown that shimmered like sunlight on a river — and slippers of pure spun gold. When she entered the ballroom, the music itself seemed to bow to her. The Prince danced with no one else, and his heart was certain at last.

But when Cinderella tried to slip away at the end of the evening, she was so quick that the Prince could not catch her. Cleverly, he had asked the palace servants to brush a little soft pitch onto the staircase, just enough to slow whoever might pass. And as Cinderella hurried down the steps, one of her golden slippers stuck fast and slipped from her foot. The Prince picked it up. It was small and dainty, and it shone like the morning sun.

The very next day the Prince rode out into the kingdom with the slipper on a velvet cushion. "I shall marry the lady whose foot fits this slipper," he declared, "and no other." Every household in the land was visited, and at last he came to the house at the edge of the forest. The two stepsisters' eyes lit up — for they had pretty feet, or so they thought. The eldest carried the slipper into her chamber and tried to push her foot inside. But her foot was much too big, and no matter how she squeezed, the slipper would not fit. Disappointed, she came out and pretended that all was well. The Prince took her up onto his horse to ride away. But as they passed the hazel tree, two white doves on the highest branch sang sweetly,

Turn and peep, turn and peep,
The slipper does not fit,
The true bride waits at home for you,
Her foot will fit it yet.

The Prince looked down and saw that her foot was indeed not the right one. So he turned his horse around and brought the eldest stepsister home. Then the second stepsister tried. She, too, could not slip her foot inside. She tried very hard, but the slipper simply would not have her. Yet she pretended bravely, and the Prince took her up onto his horse. And as they passed the hazel tree, the two doves sang again,

Turn and peep, turn and peep,
The slipper does not fit,
The true bride waits at home for you,
Her foot will fit it yet.

The Prince looked down once more and saw that the slipper was the wrong size. So he turned his horse and brought the second stepsister home, too.

"Have you no other daughter?" the Prince asked.

"No," said the father quietly. "There is only little Cinderella, the dear child my late wife left behind. But surely she cannot —"

"Bring her to me," said the Prince.

The stepmother began to fuss. "Oh, no, she is far too dusty to be seen!" But the Prince would not be put off. So Cinderella was called. She washed her hands and her face, and she came in shyly and bowed before the Prince. He held out the slipper. Cinderella sat upon a stool and gently slid her little foot inside, and the slipper fit her as though it had been made for her alone. When she lifted her eyes, the Prince saw her face clearly for the first time and cried out with joy, "This is my true bride!"

The stepmother and the two stepsisters were astonished, and at last they understood the kindness they had so long ignored. They bowed their heads and asked Cinderella to forgive them, for they were very sorry indeed. And because Cinderella's heart was as gentle as her mother's had been, she forgave them with a smile.

The Prince lifted Cinderella onto his horse and they rode away together. As they passed the hazel tree on the grave of her dear mother, the two white doves came flying down and settled softly on Cinderella's shoulders, one on the right and one on the left, and they sang,

Turn and peep, turn and peep,
The slipper fits at last,
The true bride rides beside the Prince,
The lonely days are past.

And so Cinderella was married to the Prince in the great cathedral of the kingdom, with bells ringing and flowers strewn upon every step. Her father wept tears of joy, and the two white doves sat on the church roof and sang the whole day long. From that day forward Cinderella lived in the palace with her kind Prince, and in every garden she planted she set a little hazel tree, in memory of the mother who watched over her always.

And they lived happily ever after.
